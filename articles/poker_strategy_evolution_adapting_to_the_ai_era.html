These early AI programs relied heavily on game theory optimal (GTO) strategies. GTO aims to create an unexploitable strategy, meaning that regardless of the opponent's actions, the GTO player will, in the long run, break even or profit. This approach involves complex calculations of probabilities, expected values, and ranges of hands, often resulting in unconventional plays that defied traditional poker wisdom.

One of the key concepts introduced by GTO is the importance of mixed strategies. While GTO provides a solid foundation, it's not always the most profitable approach in real-world poker games. GTO assumes that the opponent is also playing optimally, which is rarely the case. The Evolution of Poker Software

The rise of AI has also fueled the development of sophisticated poker software. These tools provide players with a wealth of information, including hand ranges, equity calculations, and simulations of various scenarios. One of the most popular types of poker software is heads-up displays (HUDs). HUDs overlay real-time statistics on the poker table, providing players with information about their opponents' playing styles, such as their aggression factor, VPIP (voluntarily put money in pot), and PFR (pre-flop raise). This information can be invaluable for making informed decisions and exploiting opponents' weaknesses.

However, the use of poker software is not without controversy. Some players argue that it gives an unfair advantage to those who can afford and understand these tools. The Psychological Battleground

While AI and software have transformed the strategic landscape of poker, the psychological aspect of the game remains as crucial as ever. Poker is not just about calculating probabilities and making optimal decisions; it's also about reading opponents, controlling emotions, and managing risk.

The ability to bluff effectively is a critical skill in poker. Bluffing involves betting or raising with a weak hand in order to deceive opponents into folding stronger hands. Successful bluffing requires careful timing, a believable story, and the ability to read opponents' reactions.

Another important psychological aspect of poker is tilt. Tilt refers to a state of emotional distress that can lead to poor decision-making. Tilt can be caused by a variety of factors, such as losing a big pot, being outplayed by an opponent, or simply experiencing a run of bad luck. Players who are on tilt often make rash decisions, chase losses, and deviate from their normal strategies.
